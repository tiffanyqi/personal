Correlations between Habits and Grades in College

[WORK IN PROGRESS]

Hi there, and welcome to my Productivity Project. Since I started college at UC Berkeley, I have been tracking my productivity through Google Calendar, then migrating the data onto a spreadsheet through a calculator application I built called CalenTools for the command line. With this information, I thought it would be fun to take all this data, analyze it, and make some conclusions about how time management, sleep, fun, grades, units, and class sizes all correlate using Google Charts.

As such, this project is an analysis of one person's life, and should not be taken as absolute fact. I found this data interesting to share, and hope that it helps you when you think that one test can make or break your grade. Check out the project below!

About Me

My name is Tiffany Qi, and I just graduated from UC Berkeley, studying Business Administration. I primarily took classes in the Undergraduate Business Administration department, but have dabbled into language-related courses, psychology, and computer science. As such, this data hinges on those specific areas, and thus is not fully comprehensive of all departments at Berkeley.

I was an above-average student with a final GPA of 3.55. The average GPA at Haas is a 3.4. I did pretty well when I first started college and received a 3.8 my first semester, then it fell pretty consistently to 3.52 until my last semester.

I studied a lot of classes outside of my major, and I typically took on heavy workloads throughout my college career. I did 42 letter-graded classes in total, 20 of which were in my major, which means more than half of the classes I took here were not business-related! Most of these classes did not fulfill general education requirements. This is an average of 17.3 units and around 5 classes per semester, whereas most students tend to take 15-16 units and around 4 classes. On top of letter-graded units, I frequently tacked on Pass/No Pass classes (such as academic interning, computer science, breadth or teaching techniques) on top of that.

I spent a lot of my time on extra-curricular activities, and comprised more than half of all my productive time. I was somewhat of a workaholic, but I enjoyed these activities considerably. Thus, while I highly enjoy my activities, I classified it in my calendar as work and productivie time. For now, "fun" is defined as time set aside for something non-work related.

I am interested in these points:

  • Time: how a student's grades and academic time commitment change over time,
  • Correlation: whether time spent, units, specific tests, or class size and the grades received in the class have any correlation,
  • Others: whether time spent having fun, sleeping, and on activities affects GPA,
  • Deflation: and whether grade deflation is really a concept at Berkeley.

While I took basic statistics in high school and in college, I do not have a background in data science or analysis. Thus, my analysis is limited to the lens of linear correlations and timescales. If you'd like to play with my data, feel free to contact me.

I will do my best to deeply analyze the data through the eyes of one student.

Grade Deflation: Does it Exist?

At UC Berkeley, grade deflation is a big thing that we stress over. The Daily Cal wrote an article about it, and shows a graph that compares the average grades of Harvard, Yale, Stanford, and UC Berkeley. There, UC Berkeley's average grade has been consistently under their counterparts. Here, I'd like to take a different approach. Does Berkeley award lower grades than our percentages in the class? Are classes at Cal naturally more difficult? Or, do the other schools just curve everyone upwards? I can't speak very much to the last question, but I can provide my own data on the first one.

Here is a chart of all of my grades and the percentages I received from each grade. Some of them are estimated, but I did my best to ask each of my professors what I received in certain areas in case the numbers were not updated online. The blue trendline shows the correlation between these two aspects, and the coefficient of determination is 0.499, which is somewhat of a strong correlation. It is surprising that it is not stronger.

If there were no grade deflation or inflation, the line should be the thicker line you see here. This was calculated by assuming the minimum points for each grade are as follows:

  • 2.70 (B-) : 80%
  • 3.00 (B) : 82%
  • 3.30 (B+) : 86%
  • 3.70 (A-) : 90%
  • 4.00 (A) : 92%

And the maximum grade are as follows:

  • 2.70 (B-) : 81.9%
  • 3.00 (B) : 85.9%
  • 3.30 (B+) : 89.9%
  • 3.70 (A-) : 91.9%
  • 4.00 (A) : 100%

I took the average of these points and extended the thickness. Thus, the red line encapsulates all possible grades from their percentages received if grades were given on such scale. The red points are these averages.

As you can see, the majority of grades received falls under the red line, which shows that in reality, many grades are given out not by the hard set percentage, but perhaps from the curve in the class. Yes, these percentages are much lower, but classes purposefully give students a higher grade due to the difficulty of the class itself. Perhaps there isn't so much of a grade deflation as we make it to be, as professors purposefully increase grades to accomodate this.

What Can You Take Away From All This?

Once again, this is solely about my data, and cannot be concluded about everyone. However, a certain grade in the class isn't just because of this-or-this, there is a wide variety of factors that play into a final grade, like how much time you study, and sleep. It's about the combination of points that create a final product. From my data, there are only some linear correlations between all these factors. And it's okay if you didn't do well on one test, it's always possible to bounce back.

Findings

TLDR; here are the results of my research.

Factors that did have a correlation:

  • Units and grades. The more units I took, the higher my grade. This doesn't appear to be a normal case and should not be taken seriously without experimentation.
  • Time and productivity. The longer I was at Berkeley, the less productive I was. My most productive moments peaked at Fall 2014, and continued to decrease.
  • Duration and grades. As time went on, the more my grades fell.
  • Extra time and grades. There is a slight positive correlation between extra time spent preparing for a class before the semester starts and the grade in that class.
  • Finals and final grades. There is somewhat of a positive correlation between the final test results and the final grade in the class.
  • Fun and productivity. The more time I had fun, the less time I spent studying. "Work hard, play hard" didn't hold true for me.
  • Activities and grades. There is a strong negative correlation between time spent on activities and semester GPA. The less I did activities, the better my GPA was. The less I did activities, the more units I took.
  • Activities and productivity. The more time I spent on activities, the less time I spent studying or going to class.

Factors that did not have a correlation:

  • Time and grades. There is no direct linear correlation between time spent and the grade received. It is possible that since I spent more time on a class because it was harder, and thus I didn't do as well, or that I spent more time and thus did better. Since both outcomes were possible, there was a very weak correlation between the two.
  • Tests and grades. In general, there is no direct correlation between specific test or assignment results and the final grade in the class.
  • Units, class sizes, and grades. There is no linear correlation between grades and units, grades and class sizes.
  • Fun, dating, and grades. There was no correlation between time spent having fun and grades. Nor did dating affect my GPA.
  • Sleeping and grades. There was no correlation between time spent sleeping and the grade received. The more I slept, the less time I spent studying though.
  • Planning and grades.There was no correlation between how much time I planned and the grade received.
  • Job hunting and grades.There was no correlation between how much time I spent preparing for jobs and the grade received.
  • Grade deflation. The majority of my grades received were frequently higher than the percentages received, but not lower.

These results display only a 2-way relationship between factors and grades. Again, all of these factors impact the final grades. All I can do is isolate the findings and compare them against each other.
